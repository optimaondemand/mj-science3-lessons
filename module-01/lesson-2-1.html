📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
A testable question is only the start. Now you need a test that's actually fair.
Last week you learned to tell observation from inference and science from pseudoscience. This week you build the actual machinery of an investigation: variables, fair tests, and what to do with results that don't go the way you expected. This is the toolkit you'll use in every single unit for the rest of the year.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to identify the independent variable, dependent variable, and controlled variables in an investigation.
2
I will be able to explain why results either support or fail to support a hypothesis, without ever claiming to have “proven” it.
3
I will be able to explain how science, politics, and economics can affect each other.
📚 PART 1 · VARIABLES AND FAIR TESTS
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
Someone tests whether music helps you study by having you study with music one night and in silence the next night, on two different subjects, at two different times of day. What's your gut reaction to this test? (No wrong answer.)

Change one thing at a time

In a fair test, you deliberately change one thing (the independent variable), you measure what happens to another thing (the dependent variable), and you keep everything else the same (the controlled variables). In the music-and-studying example, the subject, the time of day, and the material studied all changed too, so you can't tell whether music, the subject, or the time of day caused any difference in the score.

Good investigations also run repeated trials and get checked through replication. One trial could be a fluke. Ten trials with the same pattern are much harder to dismiss.

⚡ TRY IT · NAME THE VARIABLE
A student wants to know if the amount of sunlight affects how tall a bean plant grows. They put identical plants in identical soil and pots, water them the same amount, and give each pot a different number of hours of sunlight per day. After 3 weeks, they measure each plant's height.
1. What is the independent variable?
2. What is the dependent variable?
3. Which of these is a controlled variable in this investigation?
📚 PART 2 · WHAT THE EVIDENCE ACTUALLY SAYS

Science never “proves” anything

This might be the single most important habit in this whole module: scientists don't say a hypothesis is "proven." They say the results support the hypothesis, or the results fail to support it. That's not scientists being timid, it's them being honest. New evidence could always show up tomorrow that changes the picture, and language that admits that possibility keeps science self-correcting.

Here's the part most people get backwards: a hypothesis that fails to be supported isn't a failed investigation. If your data doesn't match your prediction, you just learned something real, and that often points toward a better question. Real scientific progress runs on hypotheses that turn out to be wrong just as much as ones that turn out to be right.

▶  Try it: A student hypothesizes that plants grow taller with more sunlight, but their data shows no real difference between groups. What should the student say happened?
Write your own answer first.
▶  Check your thinking
“My results fail to support my hypothesis that sunlight affects plant height” is the honest, scientific way to say it. That's not a failure. It's a real finding that could lead to a new question, maybe another factor matters more than sunlight in this range.
📚 PART 3 · SCIENCE LOOKS DIFFERENT ACROSS FIELDS

One method doesn't fit every science

A chemist can mix chemicals in a controlled lab and run the same reaction a hundred times. An astronomer studying a galaxy a billion light-years away can't run a controlled experiment on it at all, they observe, collect data from telescopes, and build models that fit the evidence. A biologist studying how species change over millions of years relies heavily on fossil evidence and comparison instead of quick repeatable trials. All three are doing real science; they're just using the methods that actually fit what they're studying.

💡 CONNECTION
You'll see this exact pattern again this year: physical science units will lean on controlled lab-style tests, while the space unit in the Spring semester will lean much more on observation, models, and technology, because you can't run a controlled experiment on a star.
📚 PART 4 · SCIENCE DOESN'T HAPPEN IN A BUBBLE

Science informs decisions, and decisions shape science

Communities, states, and countries use science to make real decisions, like where to build a hospital based on flood-risk data, or how to respond to a disease outbreak. But it runs both ways: political priorities, funding decisions, and economic pressures also affect which questions get studied and how quickly. A government that funds hurricane research heavily will produce more hurricane science than one that doesn't, regardless of which question is more scientifically interesting.

⚡ PLACE IT · SCIENCE AND SOCIETY
“A city decides where to build a new seawall based on data from ocean scientists tracking sea-level rise.” How directly does this show science informing a real decision?
