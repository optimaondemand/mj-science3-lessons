Physical vs. Chemical Change
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Melting ice and burning wood are both "changes." Only one of them makes a brand-new substance.
You've already seen physical changes all through this unit, phase changes, dissolving, breaking. Today you meet their counterpart: chemical changes, where the atoms actually rearrange into something new.

1
I will be able to differentiate between physical and chemical changes using evidence.
2
I will be able to describe how temperature can influence a chemical change.
📚 PART 1 · SAME SUBSTANCE OR SOMETHING NEW?

A physical change alters how a substance looks or is arranged, but it's still the exact same substance at the atomic level: ice melting is still H₂O, a torn piece of paper is still paper. A chemical change rearranges atoms into an entirely new substance with different properties. Common evidence of a chemical change: a color change that isn't just mixing, bubbles or gas forming, a new smell, a temperature change that isn't from an outside heat source, or something forming that won't dissolve back (a precipitate).

⚡ TRY IT · PHYSICAL OR CHEMICAL?
Classify each change.
1. Cutting a sheet of paper into smaller pieces
2. A metal nail rusting over several weeks
3. Batter baking into a cake
📚 PART 2 · TEMPERATURE SPEEDS THINGS UP

Remember from Module 2 that heat means faster-moving particles. Faster-moving particles collide more often and with more force, which is exactly what a chemical reaction needs to get started. That's why food spoils faster on a warm counter than in a cold refrigerator, and why a glow stick glows brighter (and burns out faster) when it's warm. Temperature doesn't just describe a chemical change, it actively influences how fast one happens.

⚡ PREDICT IT
Two identical antacid tablets are dropped into two cups of water, one warm and one cold. How much faster do you predict the warm-water tablet will finish fizzing?
