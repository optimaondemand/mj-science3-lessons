📚 PART 1 · THREE PARTICLES, ONE ATOM

An atom has a tiny, dense nucleus at its center, containing protons (positively charged) and neutrons (no charge). Around that nucleus, electrons (negatively charged) surround the atom in a cloud, far outside the nucleus. If an atom were the size of a football stadium, the nucleus would be about the size of a marble at the center, with the electrons swarming around the outside edges of the whole stadium. Atoms are, overwhelmingly, empty space.

📚 PART 2 · A LIMITED ALPHABET, UNLIMITED WORDS

There are fewer than 120 known elements, but those atoms combine in an enormous number of ways to make compounds. Think of elements like letters of an alphabet: there are only 26 letters in English, but they combine into hundreds of thousands of words. Two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom bond together to form a single, discrete water molecule — you can point to one individual H₂O and say "that's a water molecule." Table salt is built differently: sodium and chlorine combine in a 1:1 ratio, called a formula unit, but they don't pair off into individual molecules the way water does. Instead, sodium and chlorine atoms arrange into a repeating, alternating lattice that extends outward in every direction, so "one sodium to one chlorine" describes the ratio in that pattern, not a free-floating pair. Same handful of "letters," but not always the same kind of "word."
