Photosynthesis: Making Food from Light
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Plants don't get their mass from the soil, mostly. They build themselves out of air.
Photosynthesis is how plants (and some other organisms) capture light energy and use it to build food. Once you know the actual ingredients and products, that surprising fact about a tree's mass stops being a mystery.

📚 PART 1 · THE RECIPE FOR PHOTOSYNTHESIS

Photosynthesis takes three ingredients: carbon dioxide (from the air), water (from the roots), and light energy (from the sun), and uses a green pigment called chlorophyll to capture that light energy. The products are glucose (a sugar the plant uses as food and building material) and oxygen (released as a byproduct, the same oxygen you breathe).

💡 SOLVING THE MYSTERY
A tree's dry mass — its mass once you remove all the water, since fresh-cut wood is mostly water by weight — mostly comes from the carbon dioxide it pulls out of the air, not the soil. The plant grabs carbon atoms out of gaseous CO₂ and builds them into solid glucose, then into cellulose and wood. That "invisible gas becomes solid wood" idea has surprised scientists and students alike for centuries.
