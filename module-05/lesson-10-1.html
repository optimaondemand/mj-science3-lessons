Mixtures vs. Pure Substances
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Salt water and salt are both "matter," but only one of them is a single pure substance.
Almost everything you touch in daily life is a mixture, not a pure substance. Today you learn how to tell the difference, and why that difference actually matters for how you'd separate or identify what's in front of you.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to distinguish among pure substances, mixtures, and solutions.
2
I will be able to propose a way to separate a mixture using its parts' physical properties.
📚 PART 1 · ONE SUBSTANCE OR SEVERAL?

Classifying matter like this takes two questions, asked in order, not one three-way guess. Question 1: is it a pure substance or a mixture? A pure substance is made of only one type of element or compound, with the exact same composition all the way through, like pure gold or pure water. A mixture is two or more substances physically combined without any chemical bonding, so each part keeps its own properties and can (at least in principle) be separated back out. Question 2, only if you answered "mixture": is it evenly mixed all the way through, or not? A solution is a mixture where one substance is completely and evenly dissolved into another, like salt in water, so it looks uniform all the way through. A heterogeneous mixture is one where you can still see or separate the different parts, like trail mix.

💡 THE KEY TEST
Question 1: could I physically separate this back into its original parts, using something like filtering, evaporating, or a magnet, without any chemical reaction? If yes, it's a mixture — move to Question 2. If there's truly only one thing there, all the way through, it's a pure substance and you're done. Question 2 (mixtures only): does it look the same all the way through, with no visible separate parts? If yes, it's a solution. If you can see or separate distinct parts, it's a heterogeneous mixture. Some real household products are genuinely debatable at Question 1 (tap water, for instance, has dissolved minerals, so it's arguably a solution rather than "pure water") — when that happens, a well-reasoned answer with your evidence is worth full credit, not just picking the "expected" label.
