Acids, Bases, and Salts
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Lemon juice and soap couldn't feel more different. Chemically, they're opposites.
Acids and bases are two huge categories of compounds with distinct, testable properties. And when an acid and a base react together, they can form something in a third category entirely: a salt, and it isn't always the kind you sprinkle on food.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to identify and compare properties of acids, bases, and salts.
📚 PART 1 · ACIDS AND BASES HAVE OPPOSITE PERSONALITIES

Acids taste sour (never taste chemicals to test this, that's just how we know from common examples like lemon juice and vinegar), react with certain metals, and turn a blue litmus indicator red. Bases taste bitter, often feel slippery (like soap), and turn a red litmus indicator blue. Scientists use a scale called pH to measure how acidic or basic something is: low numbers (0-6) are acidic, 7 is neutral, and high numbers (8-14) are basic.

⚡ PLACE IT · THE PH SCALE
Lemon juice has a pH of about 2. Where does that place it on the acid-base scale?
📚 PART 2 · WHEN ACIDS AND BASES MEET: SALTS

When an acid and a base react together, they can neutralize each other and form a salt, plus water. "Salt" in chemistry doesn't just mean table salt, it's a whole category of compound. Table salt (sodium chloride) happens to form from a strong acid and a strong base reacting completely, but there are many other salts with completely different uses, from fertilizers to medicines.

▶  Try it: If you mix an acid and a base in exactly the right amounts, what happens to the resulting pH?
Think it through first.
▶  Check your thinking
The pH moves toward neutral (close to 7). The acid and base "cancel out" each other's extremes, forming a salt and water.
