Diffusion and Particle Speed
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Why does a drop of food coloring spread through hot water faster than cold water?
You already know particles move faster when a substance is hotter. Today you use that idea to predict and explain diffusion, the way particles spread out from where they're concentrated to where they're not, just from their own random motion.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I will be able to predict and explain how temperature affects the rate of diffusion, using the particle model.
📚 PART 1 · DIFFUSION, PARTICLE BY PARTICLE

Diffusion happens because particles are always moving randomly, bumping into each other and everything around them. There's no plan involved, no particle "decides" to spread out. It's simple statistics: with so many particles moving randomly, they naturally end up more spread out over time, the same way a drop of dye eventually colors an entire glass of water evenly.

⚡ PREDICT IT
A drop of food coloring is placed in a cup of hot water and an identical drop in a cup of ice water at the same time. How much faster do you predict the color will spread in the hot water?
📚 PART 2 · AN HONEST COMPLICATION: CONVECTION

Before you run your own test, here's a complication worth knowing about. Diffusion by itself is slow. Left completely undisturbed, a drop of dye spreading through still water by diffusion alone can take hours to noticeably color a whole glass. Most of what you're about to watch happen in just a few minutes isn't diffusion working alone. It's mostly convection, currents of the water itself moving because warm water is less dense than cool water. Those currents carry the dye along on top of, and much faster than, particle-level diffusion.

That doesn't make the investigation pointless. It makes it more honest. Temperature really is still speeding up diffusion at the particle level, exactly like the model predicts, but it's also driving convection currents that a simple open cup can't separate out. Naming that limitation clearly, instead of overclaiming what you measured, is part of doing the investigation well.
