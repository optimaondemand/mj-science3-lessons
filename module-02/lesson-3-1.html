The Particle Model of Matter
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Nobody has ever seen an individual atom with their own eyes. So how do we know matter is made of particles at all?
Remember Lesson 1.1's biggest idea: a scientific explanation is a model built from evidence, reasoning, and imagination, not something you can just point to. The particle model of matter is a perfect example. Scientists never directly saw an atom until incredibly recent history, but the model explains an enormous number of observations, which is exactly what makes a model worth trusting.

📚 PART 1 · THREE STATES, ONE IDEA
⚡ QUICK CHECK-IN
If you could shrink down small enough to stand inside a block of ice, what do you think you'd see the particles doing? (No wrong answer.)

Every substance is made of particles (atoms or groups of atoms) that are always in motion. What changes between a solid, a liquid, and a gas isn't the particles themselves, it's how tightly packed they are and how much they're moving. In a solid, particles are packed close together in a fixed pattern and mostly just vibrate in place. In a liquid, particles are still close together but can slide past each other, which is why liquids flow and take the shape of their container. In a gas, particles are far apart and moving fast in every direction, which is why gases expand to fill whatever space they're in.

Match each description of particle behavior to the correct state of matter.
1. Particles are packed tightly in a fixed arrangement, only vibrating in place.
2. Particles stay close together but can slide past one another, letting the substance flow.
3. Particles are far apart and moving quickly in every direction, spreading to fill their container.
📚 PART 2 · TEMPERATURE IS AVERAGE PARTICLE KINETIC ENERGY

Here's a fact that surprises a lot of people: temperature isn't a "substance" or a "thing" moving into an object, it's a measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles in that object, essentially how much motion energy they carry on average. Heat something up within one state of matter and you're giving its particles more energy, so on average they move (or vibrate) faster. Cool something down and the particles slow down. At the coldest temperature physically possible, particle motion would almost completely stop. Hang onto this "average kinetic energy" idea. It matters more than it might seem once you get to Part 3.

💡 CONNECTION
This is why a metal spoon left in hot soup gets hot all the way up the handle: fast-moving particles at the hot end bump into slower particles next to them and speed them up too, passing the energy along the whole spoon.
📚 PART 3 · PHASE CHANGES: SAME PARTICLES, DIFFERENT ARRANGEMENT

When ice melts into water, the water molecules themselves don't change, only their arrangement does. Keep warming a solid and its average kinetic energy, and its temperature, climbs steadily right up until it reaches its melting point. Then something unexpected happens: the temperature stops rising and holds flat while the solid is melting, even though you're still adding energy the whole time. That's because the added energy isn't speeding the particles up any further. It's being spent breaking the attractions that lock particles into the solid's fixed pattern, freeing them into the looser, sliding arrangement of a liquid. Only after every last bit has melted does the temperature start climbing again. The same thing happens at boiling: temperature holds flat while the added energy overcomes the attractions holding the liquid together, spreading the particles apart into a gas. Every phase change works this way. Energy goes into rearranging particles and overcoming what's holding them together, not into extra speed, which is exactly why temperature doesn't rise during one.

▶  Try it: A puddle disappears on a sunny sidewalk over a few hours, even though the water never boils. What's happening at the particle level?
Think it through first.
▶  Check your thinking
Evaporation. Even without boiling, the fastest-moving particles at the surface have enough energy to escape into the air as gas. Over time, enough particles escape that the whole puddle disappears. This is still the particle model, just happening only at the surface instead of throughout the liquid.
