Mass, Weight, and Density
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
You'd weigh about six times less on the Moon. Would you be six times less "you"?
People use "mass" and "weight" like they mean the same thing, but they don't, and the difference matters for the rest of this unit. Once you've got that sorted, you'll combine mass with volume to calculate density, the property that tells you why a bowling ball and a beach ball can be the same size and feel completely different.

📚 PART 1 · MASS DOESN'T CARE WHERE YOU ARE

Mass is the amount of matter in something, and it doesn't change no matter where that something is. Weight is the force of gravity pulling on that mass, and gravity is different in different places. The Moon's gravity is weaker than Earth's, so a rock with a certain mass would weigh less there, even though it's still exactly the same rock, made of exactly the same amount of matter.

⚡ TRY IT · MASS OR WEIGHT?
Decide whether each statement is describing mass or weight.
1. “This astronaut is made of 70 kilograms of matter, on Earth or on the Moon.”
2. “This astronaut would feel about six times lighter on the Moon than on Earth.”
📚 PART 2 · DENSITY: HOW PACKED IN IS THE MATTER?

Density tells you how much mass is packed into a given amount of space: density = mass ÷ volume. A brick and a sponge can be the same size (same volume), but the brick has way more mass packed into that space, so it's denser. Density is one of the most useful properties in this whole unit because, unlike total mass or total weight, it stays the same no matter how big or small your sample is: a teaspoon of gold and a brick of gold have the exact same density.

💡 WORKED EXAMPLE
A rock has a mass of 45 grams and a volume of 15 cm³. Density = 45 ÷ 15 = 3 g/cm³. Since water's density is 1 g/cm³, this rock (3 g/cm³) would sink.
⚡ CALCULATE IT
An object has a mass of 24 grams and a volume of 8 cm³. What is its density, and will it float or sink in water (density 1 g/cm³)?
