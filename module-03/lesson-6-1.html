📚 PART 1 · FIVE PROPERTIES, ONE FINGERPRINT

A physical property is something you can observe or measure without turning the substance into something new. Some physical properties, like mass and volume, depend on how much of a substance you have. Others — called characteristic physical properties — stay consistent for a given substance under matched conditions no matter how big or small your sample is, which makes them useful for identification. Thermal conductivity is how well a substance transfers heat (metal feels cold because it pulls heat from your hand fast). Electrical conductivity is how well a substance lets electric current pass through it. Solubility is whether a substance dissolves in another (like salt in water). Magnetism is whether a substance is attracted to a magnet (only some metals, like iron, are). Melting point and boiling point are the temperatures at which a pure substance changes state under a given, fixed pressure — consistent enough to help identify a substance, though they can shift if the pressure changes or the sample isn't pure.

⚠️ COMMON MISTAKE
A big chunk of aluminum foil and a tiny scrap of the same foil have completely different masses, but the exact same density, melting point, and conductivity — because these are characteristic physical properties. Unlike mass or volume, they don't depend on how much of the substance you have, as long as you're comparing samples under matched conditions. That's exactly what makes them useful for identification.
