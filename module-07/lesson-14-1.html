Living Things Need Matter and Energy

📚 PART 1 · TWO DIFFERENT JOBS: MATTER AND ENERGY

Living things use matter to build and repair physical structures: the atoms in the food you eat become part of your actual cells. They use energy to power activity: moving, thinking, growing, keeping warm. These are two separate jobs, but they aren't always separate processes — a lot of what living things do (like digesting a meal) handles both jobs at the same time, breaking food into usable building blocks while also releasing usable energy from it. A plant absorbs matter (carbon dioxide, water, minerals) and captures energy (from sunlight) simultaneously, but the matter and energy do different work once inside the plant.

📚 PART 2 · CONSERVATION APPLIES TO LIFE, TOO

In Module 6, you proved that matter isn't created or destroyed in a physical or chemical change, only rearranged. That exact same law applies to living systems. When you eat an apple, the atoms in that apple don't disappear: some become part of your cells (a matter pathway), some have their bonds rearranged in reactions that release usable energy, with the atoms themselves ending up in molecules like carbon dioxide and water that you exhale or excrete (an energy pathway that still has to account for every atom), and some pass through unchanged as waste. Matter is never "used up" as energy — atoms don't turn into energy, bonds between atoms do the releasing, and the atoms themselves are still there afterward, just rearranged into new molecules. Nothing about being alive breaks the Law of Conservation of Mass and Energy, living systems just move matter and energy through more complicated pathways.

💡 CONNECTION
A tree that grows to weigh hundreds of pounds didn't get that mass "from nowhere," and it mostly didn't come from the soil either. Most of a tree's dry mass — its mass once you remove all the water, since fresh-cut wood is mostly water by weight — comes from carbon dioxide gas it pulled out of the air. That single fact surprises almost everyone the first time they hear it, and you'll prove exactly how it happens in Module 8.
